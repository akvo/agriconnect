# Avocado Crop Calendar — Kenya

**For agronomist review**
Version 1.0.0 | 25 March 2026

This calendar describes the month-by-month phenology, management activities, and pest/disease risk levels for avocado production in Kenya's central/eastern highlands (800–2,100 m), the primary commercial zone. It is used by the advisory system to determine what growth stage the crop is in and what management tasks are due, so that weather-triggered advice is relevant to the time of year.

**Sources referenced throughout:**
- **COLEAD** — Sustainable Avocado Production Guide (2023)
- **BIF** — Avocado Production Manual, CDAAC Kenya (2019)
- **GRIESBACH** — Avocado Growing in Kenya, Jürgen Griesbach / ICRAF (2005)
- **KALRO** — Persea Mite Management in Avocados, Samuel Khabi / KALRO (2025)

**Important notes:**
- Timing shifts by ±2–4 weeks depending on altitude, microclimate, and year
- Calendar follows Kenya's bimodal rainfall pattern
- Fuerte and Hass are the two dominant commercial/export varieties

## Kenya Seasons

| Season | Months | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Long Rains | March – May | Main wet season. Highest disease pressure. Fuerte harvest. |
| Long Dry | June – September | Cool/dry. Hass harvest. Pruning window. Flowering begins Aug–Sep. |
| Short Rains | October – December | Secondary wet season. Hass flowering. Second planting window. |
| Short Dry | January – February | Driest period. Critical irrigation window. Planting hole preparation. |

### March — Long Rains Begin

**Growth stages:** Fuerte: **HARVEST** | Hass: fruit enlargement

| Activity | Priority | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Irrigation | Low | Rains starting — reduce or stop irrigation. Ensure drainage is functional. |
| Fertilizer | **High** | Apply CAN when soil is moist from rains (but not waterlogged). Split nitrogen into 2–3 applications. |
| Pruning | Low | Not recommended during wet season — wound infection risk. |
| Spraying | **Critical** | Apply mefenoxam soil drench (Ridomil) for Phytophthora prevention at rain onset. Apply copper fungicide for anthracnose at first dry window. |
| Pest monitoring | **High** | Fruit fly traps must be active — warm + wet conditions favour breeding. Monitor for FCM. Persea mite pressure declining (rain suppression). |
| Planting | **High** | **OPTIMAL PLANTING WINDOW** — long rains onset. Transplant seedlings once soil is deeply wetted. |
| Harvest | **Critical** | **FUERTE HARVEST BEGINS.** Conduct maturity test (7–10 days at 27°C). Harvest only on dry mornings. Pre-cool to 5–7°C within 6 hours. |

## Growth Stage Definitions

| Stage | Description | Typical Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Dormancy** | Tree at rest, minimal growth. Triggered by 2-month dry period and/or cool temperatures + short days (<20°C, <10 hours daylight). | ~8 weeks |
| **Flowering** | Inflorescences open. Type A: female morning Day 1, male afternoon Day 2. Type B: reversed. Bees are primary pollinators. Optimal temperature 20–25°C. Bees inactive below 16°C. | ~6 weeks |
| **Fruit set** | Pollinated flowers develop into small fruits. Two natural drop periods: pea-size and egg-size. Most critical water period — the 2 months after fruit set. | ~4 weeks |
| **Fruit enlargement** | Fruit grows to full size. Susceptible to cercospora (quarter to three-quarter of full size), anthracnose infection (dormant until ripening), and FCM boring. Duration varies by race: West Indian 5–8 months, Mexican 6–8 months, Guatemalan 10–15 months. | 4–10 months |
| **Harvest** | Fruit at physiological maturity. Maturity test: fruit softens without shrivelling at 27°C in 7–10 days. Harvest only in dry conditions, morning preferred. Pre-cool to 5–7°C within 6 hours. | ~8 weeks |
| **Post-harvest** | Orchard recovery. Pruning, sanitation, input preparation for next cycle. | Variable |

## Variety Profiles

### Fuerte
- **Race:** Mexican × Guatemalan | **Flower type:** B
- **Altitude range:** 1,500–2,500 m
- **Bloom to harvest:** 6–8 months
- **Peak flowering:** September–October | **Peak harvest:** March–May
- **Maturity class:** Mid-season
- **Frost tolerance:** -2.8°C
- **Susceptible to:** Cercospora, anthracnose
- **Tolerant of:** Persea mite, cold
- **Best pollinisers (A-type):** Hass, Pinkerton, Reed — ratio 1:9
- **Notes:** Long bloom period — may have more than one set of fruits on tree simultaneously. Export-quality green variety.

### Hass
- **Race:** Guatemalan | **Flower type:** A
- **Altitude range:** 800–2,100 m
- **Bloom to harvest:** 8–9 months
- **Peak flowering:** October | **Peak harvest:** June–September
- **Maturity class:** Mid-late
- **Frost tolerance:** -2.0°C
- **Susceptible to:** Sunburn (exposed fruit), Persea mite
- **Tolerant of:** Adaptable to various climates
- **Best pollinisers (B-type):** Ettinger, Bacon, Edranol, Fuerte, Zutano — ratio 1:9
- **Notes:** Primary export variety. Skin turns purple when ripe (hides lenticel damage). Pebbling largely absent at high altitude.

### Pinkerton
- **Race:** Guatemalan × Mexican | **Flower type:** A
- **Altitude range:** 800–2,100 m
- **Bloom to harvest:** 8–9 months
- **Peak harvest:** May–August
- **Maturity class:** Mid-late
- **Frost tolerance:** -2.0°C
- **Susceptible to:** Persea mite (highly susceptible)
- **Tolerant of:** Cercospora (resistant), cold
- **Notes:** Good choice where cercospora is a recurring problem.

### Puebla
- **Race:** Mexican | **Flower type:** A
- **Altitude range:** 1,500–2,500 m
- **Bloom to harvest:** 5–7 months
- **Peak harvest:** February–May
- **Maturity class:** Mid-season
- **Frost tolerance:** -5.0°C (most cold-tolerant commercially grown variety)
- **Notes:** Best suited for highest altitude sites.

### Nabal
- **Race:** Guatemalan | **Flower type:** B
- **Altitude range:** 800–2,100 m
- **Bloom to harvest:** 8–9 months
- **Peak harvest:** June–September
- **Maturity class:** Late
- **Frost tolerance:** -2.0°C

## Annual Input Schedule

### Fertilizer Rates by Tree Age

| Tree Age | CAN (g/tree/year) | DSP (g/tree/year) | MOP (g/tree/year) | Manure (kg/tree/year) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1–3 years | 125 | 225 | — | 15 |
| 4–5 years | 225 | 450 | — | 15 |
| 6–7 years | 450 | 650 | 225 | 30 |
| 8–9 years | 650 | 650 | 450 | 30 |
| 10–14 years | 900 | 1,000 | 635 | 30 |
| 15+ years | 1,300 | 1,200 | 650 | 30 |

Source: BIF Manual p.15

**Application rules:**
- Split CAN (nitrogen) into 2–3 applications per year
- Apply only to moist soil — after rain or irrigation
- Do not apply immediately before heavy rain (leaching risk)
- Phosphate (DSP) must be worked into soil to reach root zone
- Annual manure improves soil structure and offsets acidity

### Fungicide Program

| Product | Applications/season | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Phosphonate (mefenoxam / Ridomil) | 2 | First at rain onset, second 16–20 weeks later |
| Copper fungicide | Minimum 3 full cover sprays | 4-week intervals; every 14 days in extended wet weather |
| Azoxystrobin | Max 2/season | At pigeon-egg fruit size for cercospora |
| Post-harvest dip (fludioxonil + azoxystrobin or prochloraz) | Every harvest batch | Within 24 hours of delivery to packhouse |

Sources: COLEAD §6.6.1, §6.6.3

### Trap and Monitoring Requirements

| Input | Quantity | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Fruit fly traps (methyl eugenol) | 20 per acre | Year-round; replace lures monthly |
| FCM pheromone traps | 4 per acre | From pre-bloom through harvest |
| Persea mite scouting | Every 7–10 days (every 5 days in dry season) | Year-round |
| Bee hives | 2–5 per hectare | Place before flowering (Aug–Sep for Fuerte, Oct for Hass) |
| Yellow sticky traps (whitefly) | As needed | During vegetative growth |

### Polliniser Compatibility

For best fruit set, interplant A-type and B-type varieties at a 1:9 ratio.

**If your main variety is A-type (Hass, Pinkerton, Reed):**
Plant B-type pollinisers: Ettinger (best for Hass), Bacon, Edranol, Fuerte, Zutano

**If your main variety is B-type (Fuerte, Nabal):**
Plant A-type pollinisers: Hass, Pinkerton, Reed

**Note:** In Kenya, extensive overlapping within one flower type has been observed, so self-pollination does occur. However, cross-pollination with the correct polliniser significantly improves yield.

## How This Calendar Is Used by the Advisory System

This calendar is **not sent to the LLM**. It runs in the Python rule engine:

1. The system looks up the current month → determines growth stage for the farmer's variety
2. Weather rules that don't apply to the current growth stage are filtered out (e.g., no pollination warnings for Hass in March — Hass is in fruit enlargement, not flowering)
3. Management tasks marked "critical" or "high" for this month are converted into weather-gated advice:
   - "Pruning is due" + dry weather today = "Good day to prune"
   - "Pruning is due" + rain today = "Pruning is due this month — wait for a dry day"
   - "Harvest season" + wet weather = "Do NOT harvest today — wet fruit rots faster"
4. Disease/pest risk levels generate seasonal alerts when elevated
5. Only the resulting triggered advice (typically 5–10 items) goes into the LLM prompt for translation into farmer-friendly WhatsApp language
