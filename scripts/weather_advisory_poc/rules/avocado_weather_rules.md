# Avocado Weather Advisory Rules — Kenya

**For agronomist review**
Version 1.0.0 | 25 March 2026 | 75 rules total

This document describes the weather-triggered advisory rules used by our system to generate recommendations for avocado farmers in Kenya. Each rule monitors specific weather conditions and, when triggered, produces actionable advice. Please review for agronomic accuracy.

**Sources referenced throughout:**
- **COLEAD** — Sustainable Avocado Production Guide (2023, 254 pages)
- **BIF** — Avocado Production Manual, CDAAC Kenya (2019, 34 pages)
- **GRIESBACH** — Avocado Growing in Kenya, Juergen Griesbach / ICRAF (2005, 118 pages)
- **KALRO_MITE** — Persea Mite Management in Avocados, Samuel Khabi / KALRO (2025, 25 pages)

**Priority levels used:**
| Priority | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Critical | Immediate action required — significant crop or tree loss possible |
| High | Action needed within 24-48 hours |
| Medium | Monitor and act within the week |
| Low | Awareness — act when convenient |
| Opportunity | Favourable window — take advantage now |
| Informational | No action needed — background knowledge |

## 1. Diseases

### 1.1 Phytophthora Root Rot

**PHYT-001 — High Risk**
- **Triggers when:** 20+ mm rain over 72 hours AND soil temperature 15-33 C AND humidity 85%+
- **Applies to:** All stages; higher risk for newly transplanted and young trees (1-3 years)
- **Soil risk factors:** Poor drainage, clay soil, high water table
- **Why it matters:** Phytophthora cinnamomi zoospores activate and spread in waterlogged soil at 20-32.5 C. Root damage begins after just 24 hours of waterlogging. This is the most serious avocado disease worldwide.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Check and clear all drainage channels around trees immediately
  2. Do NOT irrigate — soil is already saturated
  3. Inspect root crown for dark discolouration or bark cracking
  4. If phosphonate (fosetyl-Al) injection is scheduled, proceed — wet conditions aid uptake
  5. Apply mefenoxam soil drench if not already applied this season
- **Prevention:**
  - Apply mefenoxam soil drench before onset of rainy season, repeat 16-20 weeks later
  - Plant on raised mounds/berms in heavy clay soils
  - Use resistant rootstocks: Duke 7 (for Fuerte), Thomas (for Hass)
  - Never use overhead irrigation
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.3 p189-194, BIF p17/20/23, GRIESBACH p8/17/20

**PHYT-002 — Pre-Season Prevention**
- **Triggers when:** Rainy season is 14 days or fewer away
- **Applies to:** All stages and varieties
- **Why it matters:** Zoospores become active and mobile at season onset. Prevention timing is critical.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply first mefenoxam soil drench now — should coincide with new growth before rains
  2. Ensure all drainage infrastructure is functional before rains begin
  3. Apply Ridomil (Metalaxyl) granules to soil around susceptible trees
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.3 p194, GRIESBACH p20

### 1.2 Anthracnose

**ANTH-001 — Active Infection Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 23-29 C AND humidity 90%+
- **Applies to:** Fruit enlargement, pre-harvest, harvest stages
- **Higher risk varieties:** Fuerte, Bacon, Ettinger, Edranol
- **Why it matters:** Colletotrichum gloeosporioides sporulates and infects at 23-29 C with near-100% humidity. Infections remain dormant until fruit ripens, then cause post-harvest losses up to 60%.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply copper-based fungicide (e.g. Cupravit at 50g/20L) if not sprayed in last 14 days
  2. Apply azoxystrobin at pigeon-egg fruit size (max 2 sprays/season)
  3. Apply cyprodinil-fludioxonil every 7-10 days while conditions remain favourable
  4. Prune to improve canopy airflow and reduce microhumidity
  5. Remove dead fruit mummies from canopy and ground
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.1 p180-187, BIF p23-24, GRIESBACH p20

**ANTH-002 — Spray Window (Dry Break)**
- **Triggers when:** Rain probability in next 6 hours is 15% or less AND wind under 15 km/h AND temperature 12 C+ AND daytime AND at least 4 hours since last rain
- **Applies to:** Fruit development, pre-harvest (Hass and Fuerte are highly susceptible)
- **This is an opportunity window — no risk, just a favourable moment to act.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Good window to apply copper-based fungicide — spray must dry on leaf surface for 4-6 hours
  2. Morning application is best — spray upward into canopy to cover all fruit-bearing branches
  3. Spray will be ineffective if rain occurs within 4-6 hours
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.1 p184-187, BIF p23-24

### 1.3 Cercospora Spot

**CERC-001 — Infection Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 22-28 C AND humidity 80%+
- **Applies to:** Fruit enlargement, pre-harvest (fruits susceptible from quarter to three-quarter of full size)
- **Higher risk:** Fuerte | **More resistant:** Pinkerton
- **Why it matters:** Pseudocercospora purpurea conidia require water or heavy dew to germinate. Losses up to 60-69% possible. Wind and rain spread spores.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Monitor for small irregular spots on fruits and leaves
  2. Apply copper-based fungicides every 14 days during extended wet weather
  3. Apply azoxystrobin at pigeon-egg fruit size (max 2 sprays/season)
  4. Remove dead twigs and fallen fruits to reduce inoculum
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.5 p196-202, GRIESBACH p20

### 1.4 Avocado Scab

**SCAB-001 — Infection Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Humidity 80%+ AND temperature 18-28 C
- **Applies to:** Fruit enlargement, pre-harvest
- **Higher risk:** Lula | **More resistant:** Choquette
- **Region note:** Common in humid lowlands — Kenya Coast, Nyanza, Western provinces
- **Why it matters:** Sphaceloma perseae sporulates in cool moist/warm humid conditions. Produces corky lesions on fruit surface. Spread by wind, rain, and insects.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply copper-based fungicides from end of flowering to harvest
  2. Apply azoxystrobin at pigeon-egg size, follow-up 28 days later (max 3/season)
  3. Apply Bacillus subtilis as preventive biopesticide
  4. Remove dead branches and fallen rotten fruits
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.7 p205-210, GRIESBACH p21

### 1.5 Stem End Rot

**SER-001 — Wet Harvest Risk**
- **Triggers when:** Humidity 80%+ AND temperature 20 C+ AND rain probability today 40%+
- **Applies to:** Harvest, post-harvest
- **Why it matters:** Lasiodiplodia, Neofusicoccum, and Fusarium spores spread via wind and water splash. Warm humid conditions favour sporulation. Harvesting in wet weather significantly increases stem end rot. Losses up to 30%.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Avoid harvesting today if possible — wait for dry conditions
  2. If harvest is unavoidable, apply post-harvest fungicide dip (fludioxonil + azoxystrobin) immediately
  3. Pre-cool harvested fruit to 5 C within 6 hours
  4. Minimize time between harvest and cold storage
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.6 p201-206, COLEAD 7.10 p231-233

### 1.6 Bacterial Canker

**BAC-001 — Drought Stress Trigger**
- **Triggers when:** 14+ consecutive dry days AND temperature 25 C+
- **Applies to:** Vegetative stage, young trees (1-3 years)
- **Why it matters:** Xanthomonas campestris symptoms typically appear during drought years. Drought stress and boron deficiency promote the disease.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Maintain adequate and even irrigation to prevent drought stress
  2. Check boron levels and supplement if deficient
  3. Inspect end-of-line trees (lowest water pressure) carefully
  4. Apply copper-based bactericide if symptoms appear
  5. Sterilise pruning tools between trees
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.7.1 p209-213

---

### 1.7 Verticillium Wilt

**VERT-001 — Optimal Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Soil temperature 18-24 C
- **Applies to:** Young trees (1-3 years), vegetative stage (growth stops above 30 C soil temperature)
- **Why it matters:** Verticillium albo-atrum optimum at 21 C. Causes serious damage on younger seedlings.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Monitor young trees for sudden wilting on one side
  2. Avoid planting in previously infected soils
  3. Use crop rotation with non-host crops before replanting
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.4 p193-197

## 2. Pests

### 2.1 Fruit Fly

**FF-001 — Year-Round Breeding Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 18-27 C
- **Applies to:** Fruit enlargement, pre-harvest, harvest
- **Higher risk varieties:** Simmonds, Waldin
- **Important:** EU quarantine pest
- **Why it matters:** Bactrocera dorsalis breeds year-round at 18-27 C in tropical environments. Losses can reach 100% of unprotected fruit.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Install methyl eugenol baited traps (20 per acre) for monitoring
  2. Apply spinosad or malathion-based bait sprays starting shortly after flowering
  3. Spray early morning or late evening for best efficacy
  4. Collect and destroy ALL fallen infested fruits — bag in black plastic for solar heat treatment, bury 50 cm deep, or burn
  5. Eradicate alternative host plants nearby (especially guavas)
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.1 p101-118, BIF p24, GRIESBACH p19

---

### 2.2 False Codling Moth (FCM)

**FCM-001 — Breeding Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 20-30 C AND humidity 70%+
- **Applies to:** Fruit enlargement, pre-harvest
- **Important:** EU quarantine pest; 450 degree-days per generation
- **Why it matters:** Thaumatotibia leucotreta optimum at 25 C, 80% humidity. Up to 800 eggs per female. 2-10 generations per year. Larvae bore into fruit.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Install pheromone traps (4 per acre) from pre-bloom onwards
  2. Apply methoxyfenozide at 8 and 4 weeks prior to harvest
  3. Weekly orchard sanitation of fallen fruit (reduces infestation by 75%)
  4. Apply CrleGV baculovirus spray in late evening (UV-sensitive)
  5. Apply Bacillus thuringiensis at first sign of infestation
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.2 p117-129, BIF p24-25, GRIESBACH p19

**FCM-002 — Heavy Rain Suppression**
- **Triggers when:** 30+ mm rain over 48 hours
- **This is a suppression event — heavy rainfall destroys FCM larvae and pupae.**
- **Priority:** Informational
- **What to do:**
  1. FCM pressure is temporarily reduced after heavy rain
  2. Delay next FCM spray application — natural suppression is active
  3. Resume monitoring after 7-10 dry days
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.2 p117

**FCM-003 — Biopesticide Application Window**
- **Triggers when:** Humidity 75%+ AND cloud cover 60%+ AND temperature 18-28 C
- **This is an opportunity window for biological control.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Good conditions for applying entomopathogenic fungi (Beauveria bassiana, Metarhizium anisopliae)
  2. Apply in evening for best results — UV degrades biopesticides
  3. Add sugar/molasses to CrleGV baculovirus sprays to enhance efficacy
  4. High humidity enables 80%+ reduction in single application
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.2 p127-129

### 2.3 Thrips

**THR-001 — Outbreak Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** 5+ consecutive dry days AND maximum temperature 22 C+ AND humidity 60% or below
- **Applies to:** Flowering, fruit set (critical period: young fruit up to 2 cm / 2-3 weeks after fruit set; older fruit with thicker skin is less susceptible)
- **Why it matters:** Hot dry spells during flowering/fruit set favour thrips population explosion. Damage causes scarring ("alligator skin") on young fruit, reducing market grade. Up to 80% fruit cull possible.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Scout flowers and young fruit — shake branch over white paper, count thrips
  2. If more than 5 thrips per flower cluster, apply spinosad-based product
  3. Apply abamectin when fewer than 5 immature thrips per leaf/fruit (preventive)
  4. Spray early morning when thrips are less active
  5. If rain is expected within 3 days, wait — rain suppresses thrips naturally
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.3 p128-139, BIF p25-26, GRIESBACH p19

---

### 2.4 Persea Mite

**PM-001 — Warm Dry Outbreak**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 28 C+ AND humidity 55% or below AND 5+ consecutive dry days
- **Applies to:** All stages (egg-to-adult in ~15 days at 30 C vs ~40 days at 20 C)
- **Why it matters:** Warm dry conditions drive rapid population surges. Drought-stressed trees have reduced tolerance. Severe infestations cause defoliation, exposing fruit to sunburn.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Shorten scouting interval to every 5 days
  2. Check undersides of leaves along midrib and veins for silvery webbing
  3. Action threshold: 70-100 mites per leaf or 7.5-10% leaf surface damage
  4. Release predatory mites (Neoseiulus californicus, Galendromus helveolus) at minimum 2,000 per tree
  5. Ensure adequate irrigation to maintain tree health
  6. Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides — they kill natural enemies
- **Sources:** KALRO_MITE p4-5/14/16

**PM-002 — Cold/Rainy Suppression Window**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 20 C or below AND 20+ mm rain over 7 days
- **This is a natural suppression window.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Mite populations are naturally suppressed — use this window for cultural controls
  2. Prune and thin canopy to reduce mite habitat before dry season
  3. Remove alternate host weeds (milkweed, sow thistle) near orchards
  4. Sanitise tools and equipment to prevent spread
  5. Prepare biological control inputs for dry season rebound
- **Sources:** KALRO_MITE p5/6/15

**PM-003 — Rainy-to-Dry Transition Rebound Alert**
- **Triggers when:** Rainfall trend over 7 days is declining AND temperature trend over 7 days is rising
- **Why it matters:** End of rainy season is the highest-risk window for explosive mite population rebound. Suppressed populations bounce back rapidly as conditions dry.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Begin scouting every 5-7 days immediately
  2. Pre-position biological control agents (predatory mites)
  3. Remove alternate host weeds which may harbour mites that reinvade
  4. Check for previously wind-dispersed mites on trees downwind
- **Sources:** KALRO_MITE p5/6

**PM-004 — Wind Dispersal Alert**
- **Triggers when:** Wind 20+ km/h AND humidity 60% or below
- **Why it matters:** Wind is the primary Persea mite dispersal mechanism via silk strands. Windy and dry conditions enable spread from infested to clean trees.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. After wind events, extend scouting to previously clean trees downwind
  2. Inspect orchard perimeters and alternate host plants near windward borders
  3. Enforce movement hygiene for workers, tools, and equipment
- **Sources:** KALRO_MITE p5

### 2.5 Whitefly

**WF-001 — Optimal Breeding Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 15-25 C AND humidity 75%+
- **Applies to:** Vegetative, fruit enlargement (Trialeurodes vaporariorum optimal at 19-21 C, 80% humidity)
- **Why it matters:** Honeydew from whiteflies promotes sooty mold, reducing fruit quality and marketability.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Use bright yellow sticky traps to monitor movement
  2. Apply neem-based pesticides (azadirachtin) at 7-14 day intervals
  3. Use entomopathogenic fungi (Beauveria bassiana) in high humidity conditions
  4. Rely on biological control (Encarsia, Eretmocerus parasitoids) as primary approach
  5. Remove alternate weed hosts
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.4 p138-149

---

### 2.6 Shot-Hole Borer

**SHB-001 — Optimal Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 26-35 C AND humidity 75%+
- **Applies to:** Young trees (1-3 years), vegetative stage
- **Why it matters:** Euwallacea/Xylosandrus bore into healthy wood causing branch dieback. White "sugar volcano" exudates are diagnostic.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Survey for branch dieback and sugar volcano signs in warm humid conditions
  2. Remove and burn/bury infested branches immediately
  3. Apply Beauveria bassiana as biopesticide
  4. Use quercivorol and alpha-copaene attractant traps for monitoring
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.5 p148-157

---

### 2.7 Lace Bug

**LB-001 — Warm Weather Acceleration**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 25-35 C
- **Applies to:** Young trees (1-3 years), vegetative stage
- **Why it matters:** Pseudacysta perseae life cycle completes in 21 days at 30 C. Higher temperatures accelerate multiplication.
- **Priority:** Low
- **What to do:**
  1. Do NOT apply pesticides during drought stress, wind, or freezing conditions
  2. Apply insecticidal soap or narrow-range oil
  3. Use Beauveria bassiana biopesticide
  4. Maintain mulch and proper drainage to keep trees healthy
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.6 p156-162

---

### 2.8 Scale Insects

**SC-001 — Development Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 21-24 C AND humidity 75%+
- **Applies to:** Young trees (1-3 years), vegetative, fruit enlargement
- **Why it matters:** Latania scale develops at 21-24 C, 79% humidity. Infested fruit is downgraded or rejected for export. Sooty mold from honeydew is worse in humid conditions.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply buprofezin or pyriproxyfen before first-generation crawlers move
  2. Conserve natural enemies — avoid broad-spectrum pesticides
  3. Control ant populations (ants protect scales from predators)
  4. Maintain adequate tree spacing to prevent canopy contact
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.8 p166-173, BIF p25-26, GRIESBACH p19

---

### 2.9 Stink Bug

**SB-001 — Optimal Development**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 22-28 C AND humidity 30-50%
- **Applies to:** Flowering, fruit set, fruit enlargement, pre-harvest
- **Why it matters:** Nezara viridula optimum at 25 C, 40% humidity. Causes up to 25% fruit damage. Feeding distorts fruits and causes premature drop.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply lambda-cyhalothrin early morning or late evening, not against wind
  2. Use Crotalaria rattlepod as trap-border crops
  3. Weed and prune to remove infested materials
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.7 p161-167

---

### 2.10 Broad Mite

**BM-001 — Dusty Dry Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 23-28 C AND 7+ consecutive dry days
- **Applies to:** Vegetative, flowering, fruit enlargement (fruits on shaded side especially affected; egg-to-adult in 4.1 days at 25 C)
- **Why it matters:** Dusty dry conditions promote mite outbreaks. Toxic saliva causes twisted, hardened growth, aborted blooms, and deformed fruits.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Prune to allow light penetration — broad mites are sensitive to heat/sunlight
  2. Maintain cover crops to reduce dusty conditions
  3. Apply abamectin at first signs
  4. Apply early morning or late evening
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.9 p172-177

---

### 2.11 Nematode

**NEM-001 — Optimal Reproduction Temperature**
- **Triggers when:** Soil temperature 23-29 C
- **Applies to:** Young trees (1-3 years), vegetative stage
- **Note:** Heavy rainfall suppresses nematodes (fills soil air pores) but promotes Phytophthora simultaneously
- **Why it matters:** Pratylenchus vulnus optimal reproduction at 26 C. Can suppress plant growth by 50%.
- **Priority:** Low
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply organic soil amendments (farmyard manure) to improve plant tolerance
  2. Use clean planting materials only
  3. Soil test before planting in new areas
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.10 p176-181

## 3. Climate and Weather

### 3.1 Temperature

**TEMP-001 — Heat Stress Alert**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 33 C+
- **Applies to:** All stages and varieties
- **Why it matters:** Above 33 C, avocado productivity and tree health decline. Above 35 C, fruit yield and size are severely affected. Combined with low humidity, sunburn risk increases.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Ensure adequate irrigation to reduce heat stress
  2. Shade young trees if possible
  3. Monitor for fruit drop and desiccation symptoms
  4. Do not prune — avoid exposing bark or fruit to direct sun
- **Sources:** BIF p4, COLEAD 1.2.1 p10, GRIESBACH p19

**TEMP-002 — Frost Alert**
- **Triggers when:** Minimum temperature reaches 0 C or below
- **Applies to:** All stages
- **Frost tolerance by variety:**
  - Bacon: -4.4 C
  - Fuerte: -2.8 C
  - Hass: -2.0 C
  - Pinkerton: -2.0 C
  - Edranol: -2.0 C
  - Reed: -1.1 C
  - West Indian race: 0 C (no frost tolerance at all)
- **Why it matters:** Avocado has very low frost resistance. Frost can kill trees.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Protect young trees immediately — cover with fabric or apply irrigation for thermal mass
  2. Check variety-specific frost tolerance limits before deciding on intervention level
  3. Inspect for frost damage after event — look for blackened leaves, bark splitting
- **Sources:** COLEAD 1.2 p24, 2.3 p30-39, GRIESBACH p19

**TEMP-003 — Fruit Drop from Sudden Temperature Change**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature changes by 8 C or more in 24 hours (either rising or falling)
- **Applies to:** Fruit set, fruit enlargement (critical at pea-size and egg-size)
- **Why it matters:** Sudden chilling or sudden heat triggers physiological fruit drop at two critical size stages.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Alert farmer of likely fruit drop in coming days
  2. Ensure irrigation is active to buffer temperature stress
  3. Do not apply additional stressors (pruning, heavy fertilizer)
- **Sources:** GRIESBACH p21

---

### 3.2 Wind

**WIND-001 — Strong Wind, Structural Damage Risk**
- **Triggers when:** Wind gusts reach 40+ km/h
- **Applies to:** All stages; higher risk for young trees, fruit development, pre-harvest
- **Why it matters:** Strong winds break branches, cause flower and fruit drop, and uproot young trees. Wind and rain on fruit-laden branches cause premature fruit drop and bruising.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. If harvesting, pause until wind drops below 20 km/h
  2. Check tree stakes and supports on young trees after event
  3. Collect any fallen fruit immediately — do not leave on ground (disease reservoir)
  4. Inspect for broken branches — prune clean cuts and seal wounds
- **Sources:** BIF p4/19, COLEAD 2.1 p24/3.1.2 p53, GRIESBACH p12

**WIND-002 — Hot Dry Wind, Flower Desiccation**
- **Triggers when:** Wind 25+ km/h AND temperature 30 C+ AND humidity 40% or below
- **Applies to:** Flowering
- **Why it matters:** Hot dry winds desiccate flower buds and cause abrasion, reducing pollination success and fruit set.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Alert farmer of likely poor fruit set
  2. Ensure windbreaks are intact before flowering season
  3. Increase irrigation to buffer evapotranspiration
- **Sources:** BIF p4, GRIESBACH p21

**WIND-003 — Disease Spread Risk**
- **Triggers when:** Wind 25+ km/h AND humidity 70%+
- **Applies to:** Vegetative, fruit enlargement
- **Why it matters:** Wind spreads bacterial and fungal disease spores (Xanthomonas, Colletotrichum, Sphaceloma) across the orchard.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply protective fungicides before predicted wind + rain events
  2. Inspect orchard after wind storms for new disease entry points
  3. Maintain windbreak integrity
- **Sources:** COLEAD 3.1.2 p53

---

### 3.3 Sunburn

**SUN-001 — High Radiation Risk**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 32 C+ AND humidity 40% or below AND cloud cover 20% or less
- **Applies to:** All stages; higher risk for young trees and recently pruned trees
- **Variety note:** Hass fruits are specifically susceptible to sunburn
- **Why it matters:** Sun-scorch damages stems, bark, fruit, and leaves. Most severe on the western-facing side (afternoon sun). Combined with defoliation from mite damage, sunburn risk compounds.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Whitewash exposed trunks and major limbs with 10% agricultural lime
  2. Do not hard-prune during hot season — avoid exposing canopy interior
  3. Ensure adequate irrigation to reduce heat stress
- **Sources:** BIF p9/23-24, GRIESBACH p13/14/24, COLEAD 1.2.1 p10

## 4. Water Management

### 4.1 Irrigation

**IRR-001 — Critical Post-Fruit-Set Irrigation**
- **Triggers when:** 7+ consecutive dry days during post-fruit-set stage
- **Applies to:** Fruit set, fruit enlargement
- **Why it matters:** The 2 months immediately after fruit set are the most critical water period. Moisture stress directly causes reduced fruit size, poor quality, and fruit drop.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Irrigate immediately if soil is dry — apply 40-50 cubic metres per hectare per day
  2. Use drip irrigation only — never overhead
  3. Apply no more than 50 mm per irrigation event, but irrigate frequently
  4. Monitor for leaf wilting and fruit shrinking as emergency indicators
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.1 p70, GRIESBACH p16, BIF p4/20

**IRR-002 — Kenya Dry Season Alert**
- **Triggers when:** Months of January, February, March, August, or September AND less than 10 mm rain in last 14 days
- **Applies to:** All stages
- **Why it matters:** January-March and August-September are Kenya's dry seasons. Without irrigation, trees suffer stress, poor fruit fill, and potential mortality for young trees.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Activate drip irrigation — apply 50 mm or less per event, frequently
  2. For young trees (years 1-3), use water bottles during dry period
  3. Increase soil moisture monitoring frequency
  4. Maintain mulch to reduce evaporation
- **Sources:** BIF p20, GRIESBACH p16

**IRR-003 — Flowering Induction via Deliberate Dry Period**
- **Triggers when:** 45+ consecutive dry days AND tree age 3+ years
- **This is a production management technique, not a risk.**
- **Priority:** Informational
- **What to do:**
  1. A 2-month dry period triggers flowering initiation (dormancy break)
  2. Do not irrigate during this window for bearing-age trees
  3. Monitor trees for dormancy signs — reduced flush, leaf curl
  4. Resume irrigation after dormancy to trigger flowering and off-season fruit production
- **Sources:** BIF p4/20, GRIESBACH p16

---

### 4.2 Waterlogging

**WL-001 — Waterlogging Alert**
- **Triggers when:** 40+ mm rain in 24 hours AND 60%+ chance of more rain in next 12 hours
- **Soil risk factors:** Clay soil, poor drainage, flat land
- **Why it matters:** Root damage begins after just 24 hours of waterlogging. Creates ideal conditions for Phytophthora cinnamomi. **This is the single most dangerous condition for avocado.**
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Check and clear all drainage channels immediately
  2. Do NOT irrigate
  3. Inspect root crowns for darkening or bark cracking
  4. If waterlogging persists more than 24 hours, prepare Phytophthora fungicide application
  5. Never allow standing water around tree bases
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.1.1 p70, BIF p4/12/13/17/23, GRIESBACH p19/20

## 5. Pollination

**POL-001 — Poor Pollination in Cold Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Daytime temperature 16 C or below during flowering
- **Why it matters:** Bees are much less active below 16 C. Significantly reduced pollination. Cold also delays pollen tube growth and disrupts A/B flower type synchrony.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Alert farmer of likely poor fruit set this flowering cycle
  2. Increase bee hive density near flowering trees
  3. Apply foliar boron spray before/during flowering to aid remaining pollination
  4. Postpone any pesticide applications that might further reduce bee activity
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.8 p92-93

**POL-002 — Rain During Flowering**
- **Triggers when:** 60%+ rain probability during daytime AND trees are in flowering stage
- **Note:** Occasional light showers do NOT harm flowers
- **Why it matters:** Heavy/prolonged rain reduces bee activity and washes pollen from flowers. Can lead to flower shedding and reduced cropping.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. If heavy rain predicted (more than 10 mm): alert farmer of likely poor fruit set
  2. If light showers only: no action needed
  3. After heavy rain during flowering, monitor fruit set and consider thinning later if needed
  4. Do not irrigate during or shortly after heavy rain at flowering
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.8 p93, 1.4 p11, GRIESBACH p19

**POL-003 — Optimal Pollination Window**
- **Triggers when:** Minimum temperature 20 C+ AND maximum temperature 25 C or below AND rain probability 20% or less AND wind 15 km/h or less
- **This is the ideal pollination window.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Excellent pollination conditions — ensure bee hives are in place (2-5 per hectare)
  2. Ensure A and B flower type varieties are interplanted (1:9 ratio)
  3. Do NOT spray any pesticides today — protect pollinators
  4. This is the best window for maximum fruit set
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.6.1 Table 7 p88

**POL-004 — Floral Induction Temperature Trigger**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 20 C or below AND day length 10 hours or less
- **This is a positive phenological trigger (vegetative to floral buds).**
- **Priority:** Informational
- **What to do:**
  1. Floral induction conditions are being met — flowering expected in coming weeks
  2. Prepare for flowering management: arrange bee hives, prepare pheromone traps, schedule fungicide program
  3. This is the time to apply foliar boron if levels are low (below 40 ppm)
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.7 p92

## 6. Harvest

**HRV-001 — Optimal Harvest Day**
- **Triggers when:** Rain probability 20% or less AND max temperature 25 C or below AND wind 20 km/h or less AND 12+ dry hours
- **This is an opportunity window.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Good day to harvest — dry fruit stores better and has lower disease incidence
  2. Harvest in the morning when fruit is cool
  3. Use clippers, leave 5 mm stem attached to reduce rot entry
  4. Do not stack fruit more than 2 layers in crates
  5. Move to shade immediately — pre-cool to 5-7 C within 6 hours
- **Sources:** COLEAD 7.10 p231-233, BIF p28, GRIESBACH p23-24

**HRV-002 — Wet Harvest Warning**
- **Triggers when:** 50%+ rain probability during harvest stage
- **Variety note:** Fuerte is especially susceptible to lenticel damage in wet conditions
- **Why it matters:** Water droplets on fruit accelerate post-harvest disease. Lenticel damage is more pronounced. Harvesting in wet weather increases vascular browning.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Delay harvest until dry conditions if possible
  2. If harvest is unavoidable: apply post-harvest fungicide dip (prochloraz/Sportak) within 24 hours
  3. Pre-cool immediately to 5-7 C
  4. Fuerte: highest urgency to avoid wet harvest; Hass: lenticel damage hidden by purple skin
- **Sources:** COLEAD 7.10 p231-233, BIF p28

**HRV-003 — Harvest Season Calendar (Kenya)**
- **Active during:** March through October
- **Variety-specific calendar:**
  - **Fuerte:** Flowering Sep-Oct, Harvest Mar-May
  - **Hass:** Flowering Oct, Harvest Jun-Sep
  - **Puebla:** 5-7 months from bloom
  - **Nabal:** 8-9 months from bloom
  - **Pinkerton:** 8-9 months from bloom
- **Central/Eastern Kenya main season:** March to October
- **Why it matters:** Picking before maturity results in watery, tasteless fruit. Picking too late risks quality deterioration.
- **Priority:** Informational
- **What to do:**
  1. Conduct maturity test: store a few fruits 7-10 days at room temperature (~27 C)
  2. If fruit softens without shrivelling, crop is ready for commercial harvest
  3. Match harvest timing to variety-specific calendar
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.6.1 Table 5 p88, BIF p28, GRIESBACH p22-25

## 7. Post-Harvest

**PH-001 — Field Heat Alert**
- **Triggers when:** Temperature 26 C+
- **Applies to:** Harvest, post-harvest
- **Why it matters:** Internal fruit temperature reaching 26 C accelerates ripening, ethylene production, and fungal infection onset.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Move harvested fruit to shade IMMEDIATELY — do not leave in sun
  2. Pre-cool to 5-7 C within 6 hours of harvest using freeze-blast method
  3. Cover transport vehicles with 80% shade-cloth (NOT plastic — creates warm humid environment)
  4. Do NOT use banana leaves to cover fruit (they produce ethylene)
  5. Transfer to packhouse within 2 hours of picking in hot conditions
- **Sources:** COLEAD 8.3.4 p242, 8.2 p239, BIF p28

**PH-002 — Cold Chain Requirements**
- **This applies at all times during post-harvest (no weather trigger — always active).**
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Pre-cool to 5-7 C within 6 hours of harvest
  2. Store at 5 C, 85-95% relative humidity
  3. Hot-water fungicidal treatment: 50 C for 3-5 minutes (optional Carbendazim/Bavistin in dip)
  4. Apply post-harvest fungicide dip (fludioxonil + azoxystrobin or prochloraz) within 24 hours
  5. FCM quarantine treatment for EU export: 1.1 C for 14 days
  6. Ripening room: 15-20 C (reduces rots compared to higher temperatures)
- **Sources:** COLEAD 8.3.4 p242, 8.3.5 p243-244, BIF p28-30

## 8. Spray Windows

**SPR-001 — General Spray Window (Optimal Conditions)**
- **Triggers when:** Rain probability in next 6 hours is 20% or less AND wind 15 km/h or less AND early morning or late evening
- **This is an opportunity window.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Good conditions for pesticide/fungicide application
  2. Apply early morning or late evening when wind is calm
  3. Observe wind direction before spraying — spray will drift in wind
  4. Do NOT spray during windy or freezing conditions
  5. Ensure protective clothing during application
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.5.1 p115, 6.5.6 p162, BIF p19

**SPR-002 — No-Spray During Flowering**
- **Triggers when:** Trees are in flowering stage
- **Kenya flowering:** Mainly Aug-Sep; off-season Mar-Apr
- **Why it matters:** Broad-spectrum insecticides during flowering kill pollinators (bees), directly reducing fruit set and yield.
- **Priority:** Critical
- **What to do:**
  1. Do NOT apply broad-spectrum insecticides during flowering
  2. If pest control is unavoidable, use selective/bee-safe products only
  3. Spray only in the evening after bees have retired
  4. Maintain bee hives at 2-5 per hectare
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.8 p93, GRIESBACH p6-7/19

**SPR-003 — Prophylactic Fungicide Before Wet Season**
- **Triggers when:** Rainy season is 14 days or fewer away
- **Why it matters:** Prophylactic spraying before wet periods is far more effective than reactive treatment after infection establishes.
- **Priority:** High
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply copper-based fungicide now (pre-season coverage)
  2. For anthracnose: apply from early fruit set, repeat every 14 days during wet weather
  3. For cercospora: apply from end of flowering to harvest during wet weather
  4. For Phytophthora: apply mefenoxam soil drench before rains
  5. Ensure thorough coverage of all susceptible plant parts
- **Sources:** COLEAD 6.6.1 p184-187, BIF p23-24, GRIESBACH p20

## 9. Fertilizer

**FRT-001 — Nitrogen Application (Soil Moisture Required)**
- **Triggers when:** Soil is moist AND rain probability in next 24 hours is 60% or less
- **Why it matters:** Nitrogen on dry soil volatilises and is wasted. Nitrogen before heavy rain leaches away. Both waste fertilizer and can damage crops.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply CAN/nitrogen fertilizer only when soil is moist
  2. Avoid application immediately before forecast heavy rainfall (leaching risk)
  3. If soil is dry, irrigate first then apply fertilizer
  4. Split annual nitrogen dose into 2-3 applications timed to follow rains
- **Sources:** BIF p15-16, GRIESBACH p17

**FRT-002 — Foliar Zinc (Young Leaf Flush Required)**
- **Triggers when:** Trees are in vegetative flush stage
- **Why it matters:** Zinc foliar spray is only absorbed by young leaves — application at wrong time is completely ineffective.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Apply zinc sulphate or zinc nitrate foliar spray ONLY during flush of young leaves
  2. Old leaves do not absorb foliar zinc — timing is critical
  3. Alternative: apply zinc as granules around trunk (less timing-dependent)
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.2 p75

**FRT-003 — Boron Application Before Flowering**
- **Triggers when:** Flowering is 4 weeks or fewer away
- **Note:** Especially important in high humidity/cool conditions where boron uptake is limited
- **Why it matters:** Low boron during flowering impairs fruit set. Minimum 40 ppm boron required in floral organs.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Monitor boron levels in leaf samples (take samples 2 months before flowering)
  2. If boron is below 40 ppm, apply foliar boron now
  3. Apply by fertigation with irrigation water if available
  4. Apply only when young leaves are present for foliar uptake
- **Sources:** COLEAD 1.2.3 p11, 5.8 p93

**FRT-004 — Reduce Fertilizer in Dry Season**
- **Triggers when:** 21+ consecutive dry days AND no irrigation available
- **Why it matters:** Over-application under low water availability favours pest development, causes nutrient imbalances, and increases salinity risk.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Reduce fertilizer application rates during dry periods without irrigation
  2. Avoid KCl (potassium chloride) to prevent soil salinization
  3. Combine mineral with organic inputs
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.2 p72-73

## 10. Pruning

**PRN-001 — Pruning in Dry Conditions**
- **Triggers when:** Rain probability in next 48 hours is 20% or less AND humidity 70% or below
- **Applies to:** Post-harvest (prune after harvest and before flowering; Oct-Dec in Central/Eastern Kenya)
- **Why it matters:** Pruning wounds are entry points for stem end rot and bacterial canker pathogens. Wet conditions increase infection risk at wound sites.
- **Priority:** Medium
- **What to do:**
  1. Good conditions for pruning — dry weather allows wounds to heal
  2. Sterilise pruning tools between trees (20% household bleach or 1.5% sodium hypochlorite)
  3. Whitewash exposed trunks and branches to protect against sunburn
  4. Maintain canopy height at 70% of row width, max 5.5-7 m
  5. Remove all dead and sucker branches
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.5 p83-87, BIF p19-20, GRIESBACH p14

**PRN-002 — Dense Canopy in Humid Season**
- **Triggers when:** Humidity 80%+ AND 5+ consecutive wet days
- **Note:** Dense canopy traps moisture and creates a disease-conducive microclimate
- **Why it matters:** Unpruned, dense canopy maintains high humidity at leaf and fruit surfaces, promoting fungal disease. Also reduces spray penetration.
- **Priority:** Informational
- **What to do:**
  1. If canopy is dense and not recently pruned, schedule pruning for next dry window
  2. An open canopy benefits from natural sun and air movement to prevent disease buildup
  3. Post-rain canopy flush (after high-rainfall season) increases mite habitat — prune before dry season
- **Sources:** COLEAD 5.5 p87, BIF p19-20, KALRO_MITE p15

## 11. Planting

**PLT-001 — Planting Window at Rain Onset**
- **Triggers when:** Rainy season has begun AND 20+ mm rain over last 7 days
- **Note:** Seedlings should be at least 1 m height, 6 months to 2 years old
- **This is the optimal planting window.**
- **Priority:** Opportunity
- **What to do:**
  1. Optimal time to transplant seedlings — soil is deeply wetted
  2. Water seedlings immediately after planting with 15-20 litres
  3. Mulch around base to retain moisture
  4. Whitewash stems with 10% agricultural lime to prevent sunburn
  5. Stake young trees for wind protection
- **Sources:** COLEAD 4.1 p62, BIF p13, GRIESBACH p13

## 13. Conflict Resolution

When multiple rules trigger at the same time and give contradictory advice, the system resolves them as follows:

| Conflict | Resolution |
|---|---|
| A spray rule fires, but wind is too strong | **Wind wins.** Delay spray until wind calms. Never spray above 15 km/h. |
| A spray rule fires, but trees are flowering | **Flowering wins** for insecticides. Fungicides can still be applied if bee-safe. |
| An irrigation rule fires, but soil is waterlogged | **Waterlogging wins.** Never irrigate saturated soil. |
| Harvest opportunity fires, but wet harvest warning also fires | **Wet harvest warning wins.** Delay harvest. |
| Multiple disease rules fire simultaneously | Use a single integrated spray. Copper-based fungicides cover anthracnose + cercospora + scab. |
| Persea mite rule fires alongside a broad-spectrum spray recommendation | **Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides** — they kill mite natural enemies. Use targeted/biological control instead. |

## 15. Crop Calendar Integration

The weather rules above are evaluated in combination with a monthly crop calendar that provides growth stage context, seasonal management tasks, and pest/disease risk levels. This section describes how the two systems work together.

### 15.1 How It Works

The rule engine runs in two passes:

1. **Calendar enrichment** — The current month and farmer's variety determine the growth stage (e.g., "Hass in March = fruit enlargement"). This is injected into the weather data before rule evaluation.
2. **Growth stage filtering** — Weather rules that specify `applies_to_stages` (e.g., "flowering only") are filtered out if the current stage doesn't match. This prevents irrelevant advice (e.g., no pollination warnings for Hass in March when Hass is in fruit enlargement, not flowering).
3. **Calendar-triggered rules** — Monthly management tasks from the crop calendar are converted into rules that are gated by today's weather. For example, "pruning is due this month" only fires as actionable advice if the weather is dry enough to prune safely.

### 15.3 Calendar-Triggered Management Rules

These are generated dynamically each day based on what the crop calendar says is due this month, gated by whether today's weather allows the activity.

**CAL-HARVEST — Harvest Season**
- **Generated when:** Calendar says it's harvest month for the farmer's variety
- **Weather gate:** Fires as "good conditions" if rain <1 mm and wind <20 km/h. Otherwise fires as "delay until dry" with high priority warning.
- **Actions (good day):** Harvest in the morning while cool. Use clippers, leave 5 mm stem. Pre-cool to 5-7 C within 6 hours.
- **Actions (wet day):** Do NOT harvest in wet conditions — wet fruit rots faster in storage. Wait for dry morning.

**CAL-SPRAYING — Spray Program**
- **Generated when:** Calendar says spraying is due (critical/high priority months)
- **Weather gate:** Fires as "good window" if rain <2 mm and wind <15 km/h. Otherwise fires as "no good window today".
- **Actions (good day):** Apply early morning or late evening. Ensure 4-6 dry hours after application.
- **Actions (bad day):** Rain/wind today — product will wash off or drift. Wait for calm, dry day.

**CAL-PRUNING — Pruning Window**
- **Generated when:** Calendar says pruning is due (typically Jun-Aug, post-harvest)
- **Weather gate:** Fires as "good conditions" if rain <2 mm and humidity <75%. Otherwise fires as "wait for dry day".
- **Actions (good day):** Sterilise tools between trees (20% bleach). Whitewash exposed cuts.
- **Actions (wet day):** Pruning in wet conditions risks wound infection (stem end rot, bacterial canker).

**CAL-FERTILIZER — Fertilizer Application**
- **Generated when:** Calendar says fertilizer is due (critical/high months)
- **Weather gate:** Fires as "soil is moist — apply now" if it rained today. Suppressed silently if conditions are good and rain is zero (wait for moisture). Fires as "wait for moist soil" if soil is dry.
- **Actions:** Apply CAN to moist soil. Do not apply immediately before heavy rain (leaching risk).

**CAL-IRRIGATION — Irrigation Needed**
- **Generated when:** Calendar says irrigation is critical this month (typically Jan-Mar, Aug-Sep)
- **Weather gate:** Only fires if rain <3 mm today. If it rained enough, irrigation is suppressed.
- **Actions:** Apply 40-50 m3/ha/day via drip. No more than 50 mm per event.

**CAL-PEST_MONITORING — Pest Scouting**
- **Generated when:** Calendar flags elevated pest pressure this month
- **Weather gate:** Always fires (scouting is not weather-dependent)
- **Actions:** Month-specific scouting priorities from the calendar.

**CAL-PLANTING — Planting Window**
- **Generated when:** Calendar says it's a planting month (Mar-Apr or Oct-Nov)
- **Weather gate:** Always fires during planting months
- **Actions:** Transplant seedlings once soil is deeply wetted. Water immediately with 15-20 litres.

### 15.4 Seasonal Disease Risk Alerts

Generated when the crop calendar flags disease risk as "high" or "very_high" for the current month.

**CAL-DISEASE-ALERT — Elevated Disease Risk**
- Priority escalates to "high" if any disease is at "very_high" risk
- Example (April): "Seasonal risk is HIGH for: anthracnose, phytophthora. Monitor closely. Ensure fungicide program is current (copper every 14 days in wet weather)."

### 15.5 Seasonal Pest Risk Alerts

Generated when the crop calendar flags pest risk as "high" or "rising" for the current month.

**CAL-PEST-ALERT — Elevated Pest Risk**
- Priority: Medium
- Example (July): "Seasonal pest pressure elevated for: fruit_fly, fcm, thrips, persea_mite. Increase scouting frequency. Check traps and replace lures if older than 4 weeks."
